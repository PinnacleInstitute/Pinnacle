You can deduct expenses that are “ordinary” and “necessary” for your trade, business or profession. An “ordinary” expense is one that is common and accepted in your specific trade or profession and a “necessary” expense is one that is helpful and appropriate. This is true whether you are a W-2 employee working for someone else or are self-employed, either as a sole proprietor reporting income and expenses on Schedule C of Form 1040, a partner in a partnership which files Form 1065, or an employee of your own “one-man” corporation filing either Form 1120 or Form 1120S.

If you attend conferences and seminars, meet with clients or do some other  business-related travel on behalf of your home business, it is tax deductible. However, you do have to be careful. You can only deduct expenses related to your business — no deductions for site-seeing. Additionally, you can’t deduct expenses for your family, if you bring them along. You should also make sure to document the trip, and keep good records.

Tips


A trip that is primarily for business can be deducted 100%.  This means that more than half of your time was spent on business.  Travel days are counted as business days.  A log of daily activities and business contacts are important to document these expenses.  The percentage of time you spend doing "business" on your trip is very important.  Also remember a "Day" is considered to be four hours or more doing business.  Travel on cruise ships has limitations.  Different rules apply for travel taken outside the U.S.  Please consult Publication 463 - Travel, Entertainment, and Gift expenses at www.irs.gov for more details.
