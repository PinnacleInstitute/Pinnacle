Ten Step Recruiting Sales Campaign

 
Step 1: Share The Dream

Identify potential prospects and share the benefits of the opportunity. Based on your available resources you can share the benefits of your opportunity using various methods. You can share the benefits through directly distributing collateral materials to individuals with a need for the benefits of your opportunity. Collateral materials may include the Success magazine, company literature, DVDs, brochures, flyers or your website. You may also choose to advertise your benefits to your target market using the appropriate media that targets your market like magazine ads, direct mailers, Internet ads or Internet search engine keywords.

The key to successfully promoting the product is identifying your target market and focusing your message on the benefit that they desire from the product. Do not be tempted to share benefits that they are not looking for. You must stay focused.

Measure the results of your promotion efforts to determine the best methods and return on investment for reaching your target market.

Step 2: Qualify The Prospect

Determine the level of interest and need of your potential prospect.  Contact each potential prospect immediately while their interest is still high.  Your goal is not to convince them to proceed, but to determine if they are qualified to proceed.  Do they have a need for the opportunity? What exactly is their need? Do they have sufficient resources to invest in the opportunity? This is a tricky question that you can’t ask outright.  But you can ask other questions that build rapport and indirectly answer the question like What kind of work do you do? or What line of work is your spouse in?

This step is also the time to analyze the potential prospects needs.  You must understand their specific needs and concerns before you can proceed to the next step.  You can only present a solution to their problem if you completely understand their problem.

The most successful salespeople practice the art of listening to the prospect with probing questions so that they can later apply their solution to the prospect’s specific need.  Remember, you have two ears and one mouth, use them proportionately.  Do not be tempted to do an opportunity feature dump on your potential prospect.  Listen for the benefits that they are looking for.

Step 3: Present The Solution

Support your value proposition with applicable experience and expertise. After you fully understand the prospect’s specific needs and concerns you can then present your opportunity as a solution to their needs. Of course, only if it really is a solution to their problem. You will be further ahead in reputation and integrity if you can honestly admit if your opportunity is not the right solution. Not everyone is ready to run his or her own business.

Bring in the experts if necessary to assist you with the presentation of the solution.  Solicit help from your up-line sponsors; invite your prospect to listen to an expert speaker on a conference call or at a meeting; share additional information with them in audio or video format that confirms how your opportunity can solve their problem.

Step 4: Experience The Product

Let the prospect experience the benefits of the products that your opportunity is based on.  They must believe in the products if they are going to successfully sell them. 

Use the puppy-dog sales technique; leave your product with them to try.  If it solves their problem they will fall in love with your “puppy-dog”.  Provide sufficiently brief instructions on how to use the product to maximize the benefit that they desire, but don’t overwhelm them with a detailed review of the entire owners manual.  It needs to appear as simple as possible for them to use and sell the products.

Set the expectation with them that you will get back with them in a few days to get their feedback.

Step 5: Join The Team

Invite the prospect to join your team.  Don’t be afraid to ask for their commitment.  If they express satisfaction or excitement with experiencing the product then assume the sale.  Ask them how soon they would like to get started.  “We are having a training session this Tuesday evening, would you like me to swing by and pick you up?”

If they are hesitant with the opportunity you should continue to ask probing questions to confirm that you understand their needs and expectations and that they used the product correctly.  You may need to rebuild some key elements of your marketing message. Did you enhance desire with the benefits?  Did you create rationale with the features? Did you build trust and credibility? Did you minimize risk? Did you provide a compelling offer, a reason to act now?

NOTE : After the prospect commits to joining your team, your work has just begun.  You now need to follow steps 6 – 10 to develop your new recruit into a successful independent business owner.  Remember that out of all your new recruits, a small percentage will succeed regardless of what you do; still more will fail no matter what you do; but more importantly, a very large percentage could succeed if they are given the necessary training, leadership and support.

Step 6: Assess Your Position

Assess the skills and resources, and define the needs and wants of the team member.  Whether you are assessing your own position or more appropriately, assisting your new recruit in assessing his or her position, you must know the starting point and the end objective before you can create a plan.  The starting point is defined by the available resources in time, money and skills.  The end objective is defined by the recruits wants and desires.  The level of commitment defines the aggressiveness of the plan.

First assess the available resources as high medium or low.  If there is a high amount of time and money, the options are nearly unlimited.  If there is a high amount of time and low amount of money, then the plan will call for a sufficient amount of elbow grease. A high amount of money and a low amount of time (e.g. overworked professionals), the plan may be designed to leverage purchased resources like paid advertising and administrative services. Low time and low money may be the most challenging and will require the most long-term patience.  The level of business skills will determine the amount of time that needs to be invested in training and development.

As a successful sponsor it is very important for you to understand the position of your new recruit to assist with creating the right plan for them and to set the correct expectations for them and for yourself to assist them.

Step 7: Create Your Plan

Create the action plan, activities and goals to accomplish the desired results.  Create the plan based on the position assessed in step 6.  Determine a realistic schedule for the initial training in the company, its products and its policies and procedures; and for general business training.  The initial training can be conducted in parallel with the other startup activities.  Based on the available time and money, design a plan that matches the resources as described in step 6.  The available resources and the commitment level will dictate the amount of business activity defined in the plan.

Set the specific daily, weekly and monthly goals.  Define the methods for later measuring if the plan is on track and what mid-course corrections need to be made.  Clearly define the milestones of achievement.

Step 8: Acquire The Tools

Empower the team member with the right tools for the specified action plan.  Basic tools like product and literature inventory for marketing, the business back-office website, the training and development tools, and the sales management tools will be required by every new recruit.

Other tools mandated by the created plan may include advertising sources, administrative resources, and specialized marketing tools.

Step 9: Invest In Yourself

Make the time commitment to regularly increase your skills and knowledge.  In addition to the initial training and development, each person should commit to learning something new every day.  Successful business owners never stop learning and improving themselves and their business processes. 

Regularly measure your progress and analyze how you can do it just a little bit better.  Learn from the experts who have already solved the business problems that you are facing. Do not reinvent the wheel.  Receive that little extra motivation when things get tough.
