What Is Notation?

When we say we can read music this is what we are talking about. It means we understand the language of music and can communicate by understanding and using it. Notation is the act of writing down music. We might say "Can you notate this?" or "What does this notation tell us?" The place in which we write music is a score. This could be a single line of music or a multi paged epic. We might say "This is the score for that sonata you wanted." or "We can see an example on the third page of the score."

But there is not just one form of notation as there is not one form of language but if we talk about Western music in the traditional or "Classical" format, this is what we mean by notation. This doesn't of course mean that we have to be placed within the Western world or to only play Classical music but rather as a distinction between other written or non written forms of music.

Is this form of notation the same for all styles of music? Well yes in terms of Medieval, Classical, Romantic and much 20th Century music. Yes for much pop, rock, R & B, R & R, easy listening, jazz, blues, opera, jigs, reels, hymns, ballet... the list is endless. However some 20th Century composers (see Schoenberg or Lutoslawski for example) experimented with the format of traditional notation and came up with other forms of notation. Many rock & pop composers have used their own style of notation - it is said that the Beatles could not read traditional notation and in genres such as jazz, there is often the space for improvisation or "making it up" which moves away from any written or fixed notation .

Is notation the same for all instruments? Well all instruments can use some form of notation but this changes depending upon the instrument itself. For example a non-tuned percussive instrument such as castanets, drums, cymbals and wood sticks would only read rhythmic score . Solo instruments such as flute, clarinet, violin, cello, trumpet or trombone will only read one line of pitch score whereas a piano, organ, keyboard or harpsichord for example will read two or more lines of pitch score . Guitars can read traditional score, tab or chord boxes and church bells can use pitch, letter patterns or number patterns.

So what can notation tell us? Well notation can tell us the duration of each sound or rest (rest = a lack of sound or space for silence), the pitch (pitch = high like a childs squeal or low like a roll of thunder), the volume, the overall speed , when exactly a particular sound should start and stop and any other  direction from the composer . Not all notation will contain each of these elements but it will contain at least one element . If a piece of music is complex, for example a symphony for full orchestra, the composer or at least the conductor, will want to give precise direction. A mainly improvised reel based around folk songs might only need notation of how many bars are in what key which leads us onto our next lesson...

Okay, information overload so here is a summing up:

  • Reading music means an understanding and communication via a common, written down language.
  • This language of music when written down is called notation.
  • We write notation in a score.
  • We are looking at the common, western, traditional style of notation.
  • This notation is used, at least in part, by all instruments.
  • This notation is the same for many styles and genres of music
  • Notation can comprise of rhythm, pitch, dynamics, performance directions and phrasing.
  • Notation can be as complex or as basic as a composer or conductor wishes.
