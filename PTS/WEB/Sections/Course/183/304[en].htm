CAREER CHOICE MODEL

 

In the example which follows these instructions, there are several decision categories to be used to guide the decision process which include  Factors, Numbered Options, Weighted Value, Rating, Factor Value and Total Value Per Option.

 

In the Factors column, list those factors that are your highest priority factors chosen to be used to finalize the career decision. Next choose a number from a high of ten to a low of one to be placed in the Weighted Value column for each factor.

 

Now identify the number of career choice options to be included in the decision process. In the Option column for each opportunity rate the factor from ten to one based on the degree you believe each opportunity with meet your needs. Then calculate Factor Value by multiplying the Weight by the Rating for each factor.

 

When the Factor Values have all been calculated, add this amount for each factor to reach the Total Value Per Option. Utilizing this quantitative decision making, each reviewer will be able to visualize a ranking of options and the factors influencing the decision process.

 

EXAMPLE:

 

 

 

                                                                 OPTION 1             OPTION 2

                                                                   ABC CO.                XYZ CO.

TOTAL                                  N/A            N/A       306            N/A       273

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

EVALUATE RESULTS

 

Option # 1 has over a 10% higher TOTAL VALUE than Option # 2 and appears to be the top ranked choice. Option 1 has a significantly better base with the best opportunity for salary growth and a better work environment. Option 2 has a better equity opportunity and a significantly better location. The dental/medical plan is equal for both options.

 

Further analysis of the factors included in the model should now be completed to further clarify and verify the possible decision. One of the most critical areas is full analysis of the total compensation package which may include base salary, incentive bonus or profit sharing, company equity and benefits. Developing a dollar value for each element of the package will provide a solid basis for a complete comparison of opportunities. For example:

 

BASE SALARY:  70,000

 

BONUS:                15,000

 

STOCK:                 10,000

 

BENEFITS:            18,000

 

TOTAL:                 113,000

 

The total value of the package for each opportunity can now be compared to ensure that the total value is not being overlooked. The base amount is the easiest to calculate but ball park values can be developed for the others. For example:

 

BONUS: The average % of base salary paid out over the last 3 years.

 

STOCK: The price per share awarded divided by the number of years to vest.

 

BENEFITS: The average % of salary paid for benefits by the company.

 

Another method of evaluation may include high or low risk and/or participation on long term company results. This would include ratio between base and bonus and the ratio of equity to the total package. Also, most benefit costs are pre tax and carry a higher net value.

 

Another critical area of analysis is the comparison of possible job satisfaction and future career growth. Remember that the actual job satisfaction tends to be the highest source of motivation.

 

Other factors may also include cost differences such as commuting or relocation. Also make sure that the company work environment is a reasonable fit. It makes no sense to “jump from the frying pan into the fire”. Don’t be blinded by the “grass is greener” syndrome.

 

In the last analysis, it may boil down to a feeling in your “gut” so have your significant other/others also complete the decision model and then discuss the results.
