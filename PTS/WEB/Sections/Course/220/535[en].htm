PERFECTING  PROTOCOL: 

Some Guidelines for Use by Toastmasters
 
PROTOCOL  
Protocol is manners - observing accepted practices that give recognition to officers, and show proper respect to guests and program participants. Protocol teaches the proper etiquette for recognizing and honouring those in leadership positions. It means honouring the office, regardless of the individual.

Toastmasters International is a first class organization and, our functions should also be first class. We should serve as role models to non Toastmasters and other Toastmasters regarding the correct and proper way to conduct ourselves in hosting dignitaries and other guests.

This document provides guidelines for District 42 Toastmasters to follow to ensure that proper protocol procedures are observed. This information is taken from Toastmasters resources, other experts on etiquette and protocol and from the editor's own experience and preferences.

Remember when good protocol becomes a habit, you will have the poise and confidence to face any situation with style. In being relaxed yourself, you'll relax others around you and everyone will be assured an enjoyable time!

Giving Invocations 

An invocation is a form of prayer, praise or worship distinctly religious in nature. The invocation is intended to secure blessing or favor for those present, to express thankfulness or to invite the presence of deity within the group. The speaker is speaking on behalf of the group, addressing the deity.

Some guidelines to follow are: 

 
Keep it brief - no longer than one minute. 

Keep it nonsectarian and non doctrinaire. Appeal to a universal spirituality such as: 

"Eternal spirit, we look beyond our human understanding for strength and guidance..."

Generic forms of a universal spirituality may refer to God, Father, Infinite Presence. 

 
 
Make reference to the specific occasion and the purpose for which the group has gathered. The invocation should unify, welcome and inspire the people attending. When the invocation is delivered before a meal (commonly referred to as grace), the invocation includes a blessing and thanks for the food. 

 

A four step formula may be used (The Toastmaster, Nov. 1988): 
    1. Give recognition - Recognize a supreme creator.
    2. Unify - State the purpose of the meeting and make everyone feel welcome and included as part of the event.
    3. Give thanks - Give a statement of thanks that may include the organization, its purpose, the people involved and the desired results.
    4. Release - A statement of conclusion or just an "amen" is appropriate. 

An Example:

"Giver to all things, as we gather for this banquet, we give thanks that we are again permitted to gather in this fellowship. May our friendships be strengthened and our lives be broadened. We give thanks for this food. Please bless it to our use and guide us so that we may help others be the best they can be. Amen."

Please note that an invocation is not a meditation. A meditation takes the form of contemplation or reflection. Meditation lets us reflect internally- to our inner person or higher potential. The speaker speaks as a member of a group. If a meditation is being given rather than an invocation, the Toastmaster should introduce it as such - "I will now ask Sally Jones to deliver our meditation".
 


Giving Toasts

A toast is a tribute to a person or office. There are two types of toasts: loyal toasts and social or business toasts.

SOCIAL OR BUSINESS TOASTS

 
Gives a tribute to a person. 
Preferred beverages are wine or champagne although water or juice may be used especially for nondrinkers. Ensure that non-alcoholic beverages are available for the nondrinkers. Ensure that everyone has a beverage in hand at the proper time. 
A wine goblet or other glass is used. Never drink from a bottle. 
Make the toast fit the occasion. 
Be prepared. Choose your words carefully. Refine your basic idea into a short message of goodwill and, if necessary, memorize it. Avoid clichés. 

A formula for a toast

You may use a four part formula:
  • Make a reference to the occasion or gathering.
  • Refer to the achievements of the recipient of the toast.
  • Express for the entire group, your goodwill.
  • Propose the toast formally. To do this, lift your glass and then say, "Ladies and gentlemen, please rise and drink with me a toast to..."
    •  
    Be brief - no longer than one minute. 
    Ensure the title and pronunciation of the person's name is correct. 
    For Toastmasters, include the educational level of achievement (i.e. CTM, DTM), prior to stating the person's name. 

    An example,

    "Tonight we are pleased to honor the outstanding contribution of our International President. His dedication, his commitment to the ideals of Toastmasters and his diligence in meeting the demands of his office have strengthened our organization.

    As he draws close to the end of his term, we thank him for his extraordinary contribution and wish him well in all his future endeavors. Ladies and gentlemen, please rise and drink with me a toast to our International President, Distinguished Toastmaster, ________________."

    LOYAL TOASTS

     
    Gives a tribute to an office or person. In Canada, it is to the Queen; in the United States, it is to the office of the President. 
    Propose the toast formally by: 
    - Lifting your glass and saying "Ladies and Gentlemen, please rise and drink with me a toast to the Office of the President of the United States." 
    - Lifting your glass and saying "Ladies and Gentlemen, please rise and drink with me a toast to the Queen." 
    The country representing the guests is toasted before the host country. 
    Glasses are not clicked. 

    Serving As Sargeant-At-Arms

    As Sargeant-at-Arms you are responsible for making everyone feel welcome to the specific Toastmaster function. You set the tone for the event. 
     
    Be in position at least ten minutes before the designated start time of the event. Position yourself at the microphone or designated place for the Toastmaster or host of the event. 

     
     
    Give a two minute warning to the audience to take their seats before the program begins. If necessary, give a one minute warning. 

    If the audience is still talking when you are to begin, then give the greeting (Good afternoon) and pause until the audience quiets. 

    Alternatively, you may state, "Ladies and Gentlemen, may I have your attention please." Pause until the audience quiets. Then continue with your welcome and opening remarks. 

    Introduce yourself and welcome everyone to the function. Be enthusiastic and warm. Keep your remarks brief and pertinent to the occasion. 

    For example:

    "Good afternoon, Fellow Toastmasters and Most Welcome Guests! My name is Ted Black and I'm pleased to be your Sargeant-at-Arms. Welcome to our Awards Luncheon!... "

    Introduce the Toastmaster for the event, using the guidelines for making introductions. Remain at the lectern while the Toastmaster comes forward. Greet the Toastmaster, and once he/she has taken control, quietly leave the platform and return to your seat. 

    Serving as Toastmaster

    Being a Toastmaster is one of the most important functions to be performed. The Toastmaster sets the tone for the event, acts as the genial host and facilitates the smooth flow of the program. The capabilities and conduct of the Toastmaster are fundamental to a "classy" event and ensuring that all participants enjoy themselves. Here are some basic guidelines that all Toastmasters need to follow:

    1. Preparing for the Event

    Preparation is the key to success. Ensure that you: 

     
    Know the details of the event for which you are responsible i.e.., what specific functions will be conducted, including the significance of those functions, during your portion of the program; who will be participating and who you need to introduce; when the functions are scheduled (including the start and conclusion of the program). 

     
     
    Meet the people who will be participating in your program - if a Sargeant-at-Arms (S.A.A.) will be introducing you, ensure that the S.A.A. has an appropriate introduction for you, one that fits the occasion! Meet those responsible for the toasts, invocation, guests of the head table, award presenters, etc. 

    Ensure that their needs have been met - if there are any needs or concerns to be addressed, know whom to contact to make the necessary arrangements. 

     
     
    Plan opening remarks that set an appropriate mood. Your opening remarks should make guests feel welcome and set the tone for the events to follow. 
    2. At the Event 
     
    Give your greeting and acknowledge special] guests and the audience. Always refer to the "Head Table Guests", not simply "the Head Table". 

     
     
    Acknowledge special guests in their order of importance, stating their title and name correctly, e.g.. International President Neil Wilkinson; International Director Ron Giedd; Head Table Guests; Fellow Toastmasters; Ladies and Gentlemen (or Most Welcome Guests). 

     
     
    Note: if there are a number of special guests, limit your salutation to the International positions. 
    If all Head Table guests are to be introduced, then the above may be omitted and replaced with: "Good Evening Fellow Toastmasters and Most Welcome Guests." 

     
     
    Give your opening remarks with enthusiasm and energy appropriate to the occasion. 

     
     
    Make gracious and concise introductions. See the following section on introducing speakers. 

     
     
    Learn to control and invite applause at appropriate times. Direct applause by asking people to hold applause when making a number of introductions at the same time such as that of the head table guests. 
    At other times, signal applause through your phrasing and inflection, then lead the applause. 

     
     
    Continually monitor the timing of the program events. It is your responsibility to keep the program running on time. Know (or determine) the timing for each event. If there are no timing lights (which there aren't likely to be), work out a signal in advance with each speaker to let him/her know when their time is ending. 

     
     
    In meal events, do not stall the program because some people are still eating. Rather, ask for their indulgence as you continue the program. 

     
     
    Prepare appropriate remarks before and after each event on the program. 
    3. After the Event 
     
    Make brief personal remarks about the success of the event. 

    Thank everyone for being present at the event. 

    Express goodwill for the remainder of the time together. If it is the final event of the day, then you may wish people a good night and a safe drive home. 

    Making Introductions

    HEAD TABLE GUESTS

     
    Seat the presiding officer and/or Toastmaster correctly. If there is a lectern, the presiding officer sits to the right of it. lf there is no lectern and the number of guests is even, the presiding officer sits to the right of centre. 

    If there is a Toastmaster for the event (in addition to or in place of a presiding officer), the Toastmaster is seated to the left of the lectern (unless rank demands that the person be seated to the right of the presiding officer). 

    Head table guests are seated in order of rank in one of two ways: alternate or straight line. 

    Alternate:
    The guest with highest rank is seated at the right of the presiding officer, the next highest to the left, with the rest alternating right and left. Those who have no rank but are seated at the head table, sit at the end. 

    Straight line:
    The guest with the highest rank sits on the right of the presiding officer. Those of lesser rank are seated at the end. If spouses are seated at the head table, they should sit next to their husbands or wives. 

     
     
    Guests are introduced in descending order of rank, ending with those of highest rank. Some references distinguish between the introduction of guests and speakers regarding the order in which they are introduced. Because our highest ranked guests are usually our speakers, they are introduced last. 

    However, if you are introducing guests in the audience, then you begin with the highest rank. For example, if Past International Presidents, Past International Directors and Past District Governors are in the room, then you begin with the Past International Presidents, etc. Further, these dignitaries are introduced in the order in which they held the office, ending with the most recent year.

    Ensure that the titles and names are pronounced correctly. 
    The accepted order of precedence is: 
     
    Religious dignitaries 
    Government officials (federal, provincial, city) 
    Guest speaker, if a non-member 
    International officers (President, Vice- Presidents, Directors), 
    International staff 
    Past International Officers 
    District Officers (District Governor, Lt. Governor of Education and Training, Lt. Governor of Marketing, Secretary, Treasurer, Public Relations Officer, Division Governors, Area Governors). 
    Note: All officers or chairmen of a certain level should be acknowledged if one is recognized. For example, all Division Governors, all Area Governors, Past District Governors.

    The presiding officer/Toastmaster should not leave the head table until the event has concluded and the guests are dispersing.

    Other protocol for the head table: 

     
    Arrive early for seating at the head table - especially if there is a march-in of the head table. 
    Do not draw attention to errors in seating arrangements. 
    Give your undivided attention to speakers - remember you are in full view. 
    Personal items should not be placed on the table. 
    Be prepared to stand up graciously when when being introduced, push back your chair a little before the introduction is made. 
    Guests and members do not begin to eat or drink until the presiding officer has taken the first bite or sip. 

    SPEAKERS

     
    Make your introductions brief and appropriate to the occasion. Length may vary from 30 seconds to no more than two to three minutes. 

    Learn as much as you can about the speaker and prepare your introduction. Ensure the information is current and accurate - both the speaker and the topic! 

    Stress those aspects of the speaker's background and qualifications that will establish credibility for the this audience on this topic. 

    Avoid the biographical recitation as an organizational pattern. 
    Think about the personal qualities the speaker exemplifies and build your remarks around them. An appropriate quote or question can launch your discussion of these. 

    Add a personal touch. If you know something unusual, interesting and relevant about the speaker, consider using it. Ensure that the speaker would be comfortable with you sharing this information. As a rule of thumb, unless it is pertinent to the topic, avoid references to family, etc. 

    Showcase the speaker - not your talents or personal relationships with the speaker (i.e., allowing your ego to takeover! ). 

    Don't overdo the introduction - too many superlatives, flowery adjectives or "schmaltzy" phrases can lack credibility and embarrass the speaker. 

    When MAKING THE INTRODUCTION, follow six steps:

      1. Give opening remarks that grab the audience's attention, make them aware of the significance of the upcoming speaker and set the mood for the speech.


      2. Ensure your introduction answers four questions:
        - Why this subject?
        - Why this speaker?
        - Why this audience?
        - Why this time?
         
      3. Present the speaker, welcome the speaker and pronounce her/his title and name correctly. State the name of the speaker last. At a Toastmasters event, state the educational designation that the speaker has attained prior to giving the Speaker's name.


        For example,
        "Ladies and Gentlemen, please join me in welcoming our International President.....!"

      4. Lead the applause and continue until the speaker arrives at the lectern. Greet the speaker with a firm handshake and warm smile. Return to your seat.
         
      5. Following the last words of the speaker, and when signaled by the speaker, return to the lectern, greet the speaker with a firm handshake and a warm smile.
         
      6. Express your appreciation to the speaker for the contribution to the program. Keep your remarks brief, personal and pertinent to the significance of the speaker's topic. If a recognition item is to be given, give it at this time, unless other arrangements have been made.
         
      Alternate:
      The guest with highest rank is seated at the right of the presiding officer, the next highest to the left, with the rest alternating right and left. Those who have no rank but are seated at the head table, sit at the end.

      Straight line:
      The guest with the highest rank sits on the right of the presiding officer. Those of lesser rank are seated at the end. If spouses are seated at the head table, they should sit next to their husbands or wives.

      Displaying the Canadian Flag and Other Flags

      At all times the flag must be treated with dignity and respect and flown or displayed properly. The Flag should be in good repair. All Flags being displayed should be of the same size. The following protocol is to be observed: 
     
    The flag may be displayed flat or flown on a staff. If flat, it may be hung horizontally or vertically. If it hangs vertically against a wall, the flag should be placed so that the upper part of the leaf is to the left and the stem is to the right as seen by spectators. 

    When used on a speaker's platform, the flag should be flown to the right of the speaker. 

    When used in the body of an auditorium, the flag should be flown to the right of the audience. 

    In a procession, where several flags are carried, the Canadian flag is in the position of honour at the marching right or at the centre front. 

    The Canadian Flag is given the place of honour when flown or displayed with other flags: 

    When two or more than three flags are flown together, the Canadian Flag should be on the left as seen by spectators in front of the flags. If a number of countries are represented, the Canadian flag may be flown at each end of a line of flags. 

    When three flags are flown together, the Canadian Flag should occupy the central position, with the next ranking flag to the left and the third ranking flag to the right, as seen by spectators in front of the flags. In our Toastmasters functions, the Canadian flag would be central, the United States flag to the left and the Alberta flag to the right. If the Saskatchewan flag is also flown, follow the procedures above for more than three flags. 

    The National Anthem

     
    The National Anthem of the country of special guests is played first, followed by the anthem of the host country. 
    Rise, face the flag and remain standing at attention during the National Anthem, including that of the guest country. 
    Memorize the words and sing to your own national Anthem. It is optional if you choose to sing to a foreign country's anthem. 
    Do not smoke, chew gum or talk while the anthem is in progress. 
    Men should remove their hats. 

     


    Serving as Host for a Dignitary

    When you are assigned as a host for a dignitary , you are acting as a diplomat for the Toastmasters organization. Your conduct is critical to the impression made about the organization. The following guidelines will help to make your job enjoyable and easier while ensuring that proper protocol is observed.

    Be on time, preferably ahead of time, to greet your guests. Stand near the place of arrival. If you have not met the guests before, when they arrive, go forward, extend your hand, and state their name(s) , then introduce yourself.

    For example,
    "Mr. Black? (Wait for the response.) How do you do? My name is Sally Smart (if present, introduce your escort too). I'm (We're) pleased to be your host(s) for this evening. Welcome to our banquet.... "

    Escort your guests to the cloak room or area where coats etc., may be left. Alternatively, take your guest's coat to the cloakroom. If your guest has traveled a distance, s/he may wish to visit the washroom/powder room before meeting other guests.

    If the guest is present during the cocktail hour, offer a cocktail to your guest, then retrieve the same. Arrange for complimentary cocktail tickets ahead of time with the Convention Committee. Never leave your guest alone. Ensure that your guest has met at least one other person(s) before you retrieve cocktails or look after any other needs.

    Introduce your guest to a few people at a time. Ensure the guest is introduced to the Toastmaster and other dignitaries who will be joining the guest, such as the international Director. Ensure the name and title of your guest is given correctly.

    In making introductions, observe the following:

     
    One person is always introduced to another person by saying the name of the person to whom the other is being introduced. For example, "Mrs. Smith, I'd like you to meet Mr. Black." 
    Use a friendly relaxed manner when Introducing people. Remember to smile. 
    Mention both the first and last names distinctly, including titles. Note: do not use first names in the following situations unless specifically requested to do so: 
    To a superior in one's business, 
    To a business client or customer, 
    To a person of higher rank, 
    To professional people offering their services, 
    To an older person. 
    Include an item of interest to facilitate further conversation. The item may pertain to the reason the person is a special guest, a particular accomplishment, hobby, etc. As with any introductions, be discreet and do not say anything that would embarrass the guest or those being introduced.

    Know how to handle different introductory situations:

     
    The younger person is introduced to the older person. 
    A man is introduced to a woman. 
    A less important person is introduced To a more important person. 
    A younger couple is introduced to an older couple. 
    An untitled person is introduced to a titled person; For example, "Mr. President, may I present Mr. Black". 
    Use titles, unless requested not to, Such as in the case of a doctor. 
    For live-togethers, give each person's Full name - no explanation need be given. 
    For husbands and wives with different names, introduce the wife first and give their full names. 
    For example, "Anne Walker and her husband John Smith." 
    If you forget someone's name, apologize briefly and wait for the persons involved to volunteer their names. 
    Make conversation with your guest. If you do not know your guest, learn some information in advance to allow you to make conversation. Avoid sensitive, personal or embarrassing topics. 
     
    Suitable topics include hobbies and subjects where the guest has expertise. Local news stories may be of interest, but be cautious when discussing any political or religious stories - the best rule of thumb is to avoid such topics, unless the guest brings up the subject, then resort to listening to the guest's opinions on the topics and be cautious in offering your own! 

    If you don't know some information about a topic, or a question asked, say you don't know rather than giving wrong information or saying something silly. 

    Do not gossip. Remember, great minds discuss ideas and events. 

    Explain or clarify the proceedings to the guest for the event. 
    Escort the guest to the appropriate place for the function. This may involve a march-in for the Head Table, or place at a separate table of honour. If you are seated with your guest, ensure that the guest is served first. Be alert to any needs that your guest may have.

    Before the event concludes, thank your guest for being in attendance, and offer compliments or congratulations for their role in the event. Let them know how much you appreciated their attendance and getting to know them. Wish them well. Escort your guest to the door and assist in retrieving coats, etc.

    Ensure that transportation arrangements have been made for the guest, if they are required.
