Copy and Move Sheets
Step 1: Move a Sheet

Click the sheet tab of the worksheet you want to move and then press and hold the mouse button. The pointer will change to a small sheet.
Step 2: Select Location

Drag the pointer/sheet to the right or in front of the sheet tab where you want to move the worksheet.
Step 3: Copy a Sheet

Click the sheet tab of the worksheet you want to copy. Click the Edit menu and select Move or Copy Sheet. If you want to copy the sheet to another workbook, click the To Book pop-up menu and select that workbook. The sheets of the selected workbook appear in the Before Sheet Box.
Step 4: Click a Sheet Name

Click a sheet name in the Before Sheet list box. The copy will be inserted before this sheet. Click the Create a Copy check box and click OK.

Insert Worksheets
Step 1: Intro

Inserting is different from copying or moving an existing sheet, because you are inserting a brand new sheet. When you insert a sheet, it is placed before the current active sheet.
Step 2: Use the Menu bar

Select Insert and choose Worksheet from the menu bar.
Step 3: Use the Shortcut Menu

Right click the sheet’s tab and select Insert from the popup menu that appears.
Step 4: Select Worksheet

Select the worksheet icon in the Insert dialog box that appears.

Delete Worksheets
Step 1: Delete One Worksheet

To delete a worksheet, simply select the sheet you want to delete. Select Edit and choose Delete Sheet from the menu bar.
Step 2: Delete Several Worksheets

To delete multiple sheets, first group them by clicking on the tab of the first sheet you want to delete, holding down the Ctrl key and then selecting other sheets by clicking on their sheet tabs.
Step 3: Be Careful

You will want to be very careful about selecting multiple sheets for deletion, as you will not be prompted to verify each sheet’s deletion, only the deletion of the group as a whole.
Step 4: Use the Shortcut Menu

To delete a sheet with the sheet’s shortcut menu, just right click the sheet’s tab and select Delete from the popup menu that appears.

Rename Sheets
Step 1: Intro

You can immediately make your worksheets more useful by naming them, as it gets hard to keep track of which Sheet1 contains the data you really want.
Step 2: Select a Sheet

Select the sheet you want to rename and double click or right click on the sheet tab and select Rename.
Step 3: Enter the Name

Enter the name you want, up to 31 characters and press enter. Remember, you can’t use the following characters in the worksheet name: slash (/), backslash (\), question mark (?), colon (:) or asterisk (*).
Step 4: Worksheet is Renamed

Your renamed worksheet should now have its new name in the tab.

Create a Simple Formula
Step 1: Select a Cell

Click the cell where you want to enter the formula and type an equal sign (=).
Step 2: Build the Formula

Click the first of the cells you want to add to the formula. The cell's reference is immediately added to the equation. Type an operator for the formula, such as a plus symbol (+), or use another operator: subtract (-), multiply (*), or divide (/).
Step 3: Finish the Formula

Click the next cell you want to add to the formula. As you click each cell, its cell address, or cell reference appears in the formula.
Step 4: Press Enter

Press Enter to complete the formula. The formula is entered, and the result appears in the formula cell. The formula itself is displayed in the Formula bar. To view the formula, click the cell containing the formula results.
Step 5: Test the Formula

Now change the values in the cells you referenced in the formula; the formula result changes automatically because the formula adds whichever values are in the cells.

Fix Formula Errors
Step 1: Select Cells to Move

Select the cell or cells you want to examine for accuracy.
Step 2: Open the Auditing Submenu

Open the Tools menu and select Formula Auditing, then choose Trace Precedents.
Step 3: Trace the Precedents

Excel immediately marks the cell references used in the formula in blue and traces them back to the cell you selected in step 1 with a blue arrow.
Step 4: Remove the Trace

To turn off the trace marks, open the Tools menu and select Formula Auditing, then Remove All Arrows.
Step 5: Trace Errors

If your worksheet cell displays an error result, use the Trace Error auditing tool to help you track down the source of the error. Select the cell and then open the Tools menu.Choose Formula Auditing, then Trace Error.

Use AutoSum
Step 1: Enter the Numbers

Enter the numbers you want to sum in a column, row, or rectangular block of cells. The formula ignores any text entries in the range of cells (the collection of cells you will sum).
Step 2: Select a Location for the Sum

Select the cell where you want the sum result to appear (usually at the end of the row or column of numbers).
Step 3: Click AutoSum

On the Standard toolbar, click the AutoSum button.
Step 4: The SUM Formula is Entered

The AutoSum button inserts a formula that uses the SUM function and surrounds the cells being summed with a flashing border.
Step 5: Complete the Entry

If the flashing border is surrounding all the cells you want to sum, press Enter to complete the formula. If the surrounded cells are wrong, drag to select the cells you want summed (the flashing border surrounds the cells you drag), and press Enter. The result of the formula is displayed in the cell.
Step 6: The Formula and the Result

To see the formula and the result, click the cell where you entered the formula. The results appear in the cell, and the formula appears in the Formula bar.

Auto Calculate
Step 1: Select Cells to Calculate

Select the cells you want to calculate.
Step 2: Look at Auto Calculate

Look at the Auto Calculate box on the status bar. The calculation result is displayed for the selected cells.
Step 3: See the Auto Calculate Functions

To change the calculation function, right-click the Auto Calculate box on the status bar.
Step 4: Select a Function

Click a different function, or click None to turn it off.
Step 5: Look at the Results

Auto Calculate changes to show the results of your selected function.

Enter Functions
Step 1: Type =

Click the cell in which you want to write the formula and type an equal sign (=). On the Formula bar, click the down arrow next to the Name box. The Name box becomes a list of common function names.
Step 2: Select a Function

Click a function name from the list box. Here we will demonstrate the Average function.
Step 3: Use the Formula Palette

The Formula palette appears. The palette may guess which cells you want to average, but you can select exactly which cells to use with the function if Excel guesses incorrectly.
Step 4: Drag the Cells to Be Calculated

If the correct cells you want to calculate aren't listed in the Number1 argument box, drag over the cell references in the worksheet to highlight them. A moving border appears around the cells, and the correct cell references now appear in the Formula palette's Number1 argument box.
Step 5: Complete the Formula

Click OK to complete the formula. The palette disappears, and the formula result appears in the worksheet. The formula itself, including the function, appears in the Formula bar.
Step 6: Try the Other Functions

The MIN, MAX, and COUNT functions work the same way as the AVERAGE and SUM functions. If you don't see the function you want on the Functions list, click More Functions and find the function in the Paste Function dialog box.

Work With Cell Addresses
Step 1: Enter a Formula

To demonstrate the difference between relative and absolute references, enter a formula that sums the two cells left of the formula.
Step 2: AutoFill the Formula

Copy the formula down the column using AutoFill. The formula for each cell adjusts to sum the two cells to its left. Usually, this is exactly what you want.
Step 3: Rewrite the Formula

Now delete the formulas and rewrite the original, so it multiplies the cell to its left by the value in cell F2.
Step 4: AutoFill the Formula

Use AutoFill to copy the formula down the column again. The results are wrong because relative references tell each formula to multiply the cell to its left by the cell on its right (instead of the value in F2). You need to change the F2 reference to an absolute reference so that each formula multiplies the cell on its left by the value in cell F2.
Step 5: Change the Reference Type

To change the reference type, click the cell that contains the original formula. In the Formula bar, click the reference D2, and press D4. (Pressing F4 cycles the reference type repeatedly through all the reference types). When the reference in the Formula bar reads $F$2, press Enter to complete the formula.
Step 6: AutoFill the Formula

Use AutoFill to copy the formula down the column again. Now each formula multiplies the cell on its left by the value in cell F2.

Use the Chart Wizard
Step 1: Select Data

Select the table or list of data you want to chart. Include headings and labels, but don't include subtotals or totals.
Step 2: Start the Chart Wizard

On the Standard toolbar, click the Chart Wizard button. The Chart Wizard starts (if the Office Assistant shows up, click No, I don't need help now to send him away). Click the Chart type you want on the left and the Chart sub-type you want on the right, and then click Next.
Step 3: Check the Data Range

Check the Data range to be sure it's correct. Click the two Series in options (Rows and Columns) to see which layout is best, and then click Next.
Step 4: Set Chart Features

Type a Chart title and axis titles if you want them on the Titles tab. You can reposition or turn off the legend on the Legend tab, and append a data table to the bottom of the chart by clicking the Show data table check box on the Data Table tab. Click Next.
Step 5: Finish the Chart

Choose a location for the chart, the As object in option creates an embedded chart object on the worksheet you select from the As object in drop-down list. The As new sheet option creates a separate chart sheet (this is similar to a worksheet, but it only holds a big chart) in the workbook. Then click Finish.
Step 6: Resize and Move the Chart

To resize a chart object on a worksheet, click it and drag one of the handles that appear around its edges. To resize a chart without stretching it out of proportion, hold down Shift while you drag a corner handle. To move the chart, click anywhere near its edge and drag it. To deselect the chart and return to the worksheet, click in the worksheet.

Change the Chart Type
Step 1: Select the Chart

Click the chart to select it. If the chart is on a chart sheet, it is automatically selected when you click its sheet tab.
Step 2: Click the Chart Type Button

On the Chart toolbar, click the down arrow on the Chart Type button. Click a chart type icon on the button's list.
Step 3: The New Chart Type is Applied

The chart type changes to the type you select (shown is the same column chart changed to a pie chart).
Step 4: Use the Chart Type Command

With the chart selected, choose Chart, Chart Type. On the Chart Types dialog box, select a Chart type and Chart sub-type, and then choose OK (shown is the same chart changed to a 3D bar chart).
Step 5: Change Markers for Single Series

To change the chart type for a single series in a multi-series chart, right-click one of the data markers in the series and then choose Chart Type.
Step 6: Select a Different Marker Type

On the Chart Types dialog box, select a new chart type for the selected series, and then choose OK.

Chart and Axis Titles
Step 1: Move a Title

To move a title, click to select it, and then drag its border.
Step 2: Change a Title's Text

Click the title to select it; when you point at the title text, the mouse pointer becomes a cursor. Drag to select the characters you want to change or delete, or click to place the insertion point within the title and type new characters (when you click or drag within the title text, the title's border disappears). Click anywhere outside the title to finish.
Step 3: Delete a Title

To delete a title, click the title to select it, and then press Delete. You can also right-click the title and choose Clear.
Step 4: Add a Title

To add a new chart title, choose Chart, and select Chart Options. On the Titles tab of the Chart Options dialog box, type your title and choose OK.
Step 5: Use a Text Box

To add a text box as a title, select the chart, type your title text, and press Enter. As you type the text appears in the Formula bar. After you press Enter, the text box appears on the chart. Move the text box by dragging its border. Text boxes are similar to titles, except you can resize a text box.
Step 6: Change Colors

To format the colors in either a title or a text box, select the object and choose colors from the Fill Color and Font Color buttons on the Formatting toolbar. Or right-click the object, choose Format Chart Title or Format Text Box, and set formatting details in the dialog boxes.
Step 7: Change the Font or Font Size

To change the font or font size in either a title or a text box, select the object and then drag to select the characters you want to change. Make changes in the Font and Font Size boxes on the Formatting toolbar; or right-click the object, click Format Chart Title or Format Text Box, and set formatting details in the dialog boxes.

Change Chart Data
Step 1: Drag the Source Range Border

Click the chart to select it. The source data is surrounded by a colored border; drag the corner handle of the colored border to expand (or reduce) the source data range.
Step 2: Drag and Drop Data on the Chart

Select the new data on the worksheet and drag the range border as if you were dragging and dropping to move it, but drag it onto the chart and drop it anywhere. When you drag the mouse pointer onto the chart, it acquires a small plus symbol; when you release the mouse pointer the data is added to the chart.
Step 3: Use the Add Data Command

Select the chart, choose Chart and select Add Data. The Add Data dialog box appears.
Step 4: Drag the New Data

Drag to select the data on the worksheet you want to add (the added range appears in the Add Data dialog box), and choose OK.
Step 5: Use the Source Data Command

Use this method if you want to completely change the source range. Right-click in the chart area and choose Source Data.

Change the Chart Background
Step 1: Open the Shortcut Menu

Select the chart, and then right-click and select Format Chart Area from the shortcut menu.
Step 2: Display the Patterns Tab

Click the Patterns tab to bring it to the front of the dialog box. By default, Excel automatically adds a border to your chart. To specify any additional border formatting, use the Style, Color, and Weight drop-down arrows.
Step 3: Add a Background Color

Under the Area options, choose a new background color from the palette. To select a background pattern, click the Fill Effects button to open the Fill Effects dialog box.
Step 4: The Fill Effects Dialog Box

To set a pattern, click the Pattern tab and choose a pattern to use. You can also use the Gradient and Texture tabs to create other background fill effects. Click OK to exit the dialog box and return to the Format Chart Area dialog box.
Step 5: Apply the New Settings

Click OK to apply the new background color, border, or pattern to the chart.
Step 6: New Chart Background

Based on your selections in the previous steps, your Excel chart now exhibits a new background color, pattern, or border.
