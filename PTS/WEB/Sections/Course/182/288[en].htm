The Pronunciation

Pronouncing Umlauts

You may have seen those peske little dots that sometimes appear over vowles in German words. They are called Umlaute (umlauts).
They slightly alter the sound of a vowel, as outlined:



German letter As in English German Word
ä (long) hair nächste
ä (short) let Bäcker
ö learn hören
ü lure Tür




Pronouncing Diphtongs

Diphthongs are combinations of two vowles in one syllable (as in English "lie"), and the German language has quite a few of them, as shown here:



German Diphtongs As in English German Word
ai cry Mais
au now laut
äu / eu boy Häuser / Leute
ei cry ein / mein
ie deer Liebe




Pronouncing Combinations of consonants

The German language has a few combinations of consonants that don't occur in the English language. Most of them are easy to pronunce, with the exception of "ch", which is unfamiliar to the English tongue.

Pronouncing "ch"

ch The letter combination ch is the trickiest one in the German language and the most difficult one to learn for English speakers. There is absolutely no equivalent for it in English and you actually have to learn a new sound - a kind of gargeling hiss - in order to say it.

Try to approximate this sound by starting with the way you pronunce the letter "h" in the beginning of the word "human" and then drawing out and emphasizing the "h".

The "ch" sound is produced at the same place in the back of your throat as the "k" sound. But instead of rolling your tongue in the back of your mouth - as you do when you pronunce a "k" - you have to lower it and bring it forward to your front teeth.
Very easy, isn't it? :o)
